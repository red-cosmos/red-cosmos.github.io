Socialism or Collapse
The Predicament of Humanity in the 21st Century

In the spring of 1896, a paper by a Swedish scientist was published in a journal based in London. It was titled, ‘On the Influence of Carbonic Acid in the Air upon the Temperature of the Ground’. The scientist’s name was Svante Arrhenius. It discussed, at a time when the polio vaccine had not yet been developed, how increasing concentrations of carbon dioxide gas (then referred to as ‘carbonic acid’) in the atmosphere could lead to a rise in the average temperature of the earth’s surface. He estimated that a 2.5 fold increase in the atmospheric CO₂ concentration would result in an 8-9°C increase in the temperature of the arctic.1 This was one of the first quantifications of the greenhouse effect and marked the early days of the climate change awareness movement.

Twenty-five years before Arrhenius’s paper, another movement was seeing its beginnings. After the Prussian army forced the Provisional Government of Paris – the same Government that violently massacred protesting Parisians a few days prior – to surrender, most of Paris was disarmed. The National Guard and a large portion of the working class, as they kept their weapons, restricted the Prussians to only small sections of the city. The National Assembly, having now moved to Versailles, sent in the army to destroy the workers’ movement, but after they refused to carry out their orders and stood in solidarity with the workers, the Paris Commune was elected by the citizens of Paris.2 A highly democratic enterprise, the Commune weathered two months of attacks and sporadic attempts at sabotage before being brutally suppressed by a French army assisted by the Prussians. More than a week was spent by the army – the Bloody Week – murdering workers, shooting civilians on sight.

While, in Europe, the working class was creating revolutionary change, in Asia, the British Crown, in 1857, was taking control of India from the East India Company. Over the course of the next 90 years, a river of wealth flowed from Delhi to London.3 India was being deindustrialised, impoverished, and, to make sure she could never catch up to her former masters, divided on religious lines. Meanwhile, other European giants were playing different flavours of the same game in various parts of Africa, Southeast Asia and South America, taking home the wealth and leaving behind deformed ‘states’ with borders drawn at the whim of some military officer. But at the start of the 20th century, when the behemoth of colonialism was selfishly devouring most of the world, a new kind of society was born in Russia, one similar to that of the Communards, but bigger in scope and more resilient in the face of adversity. After successfully defending the country from a military intervention by the imperialists,4 The Union of Soviet Socialist Republics would stand tall for 70 years before its untimely dissolution.

As the communists worked their way through a civil war and a world war, fighting tooth and nail for mere survival, the capitalists were using all the resources they could get their hands on to enrich themselves while loudly proclaiming ideals such as equality and freedom.5 As it became more and more expensive to maintain factories and pay workers in the first world, more and more of these countries started moving the manufacturing jobs to the global south, to the ‘developing’ countries, where they could exploit the cheap labour, salvage whatever resources were left after decades of being colonies, and, as a bonus, distance themselves from most of the environmental damage.6 If we carefully look at this process, we can see it for what it really is: the conversion of a very fragile biosphere, with the help of the labour of the already impoverished, into chunks of paper called money, or – just as bad – numbers on a few redundant computer harddisks sitting in distributed data vaults located anywhere from Zurich to Mumbai. And to maintain this process, the accumulation of capital for the sake of itself, it needs undisrupted economic growth.

From the daily experience of a human, growth is a welcome phenomenon. Seeds grow into trees, babies grow into adults. Yet when blood cells develop a will of their own and begin multiplying indefinitely, it is considered but a deadly disease. If the world economy grew at a rate of only 5% every year, its size would have to double every 14 years. In this steady growth scenario, as much resources would be consumed and as much pollution would result every 14 years as that of all the previous years combined. In other words, we would consume and pollute as much from 2020 to 2034 as we did in all of modern history. This is neither healthy, nor sustainable.7 The capitalist concept of economic growth is less like a tree and more like cancer. People long before Arrhenius have been aware of this, but it has rarely trickled into mainstream thought because of various factors.

One important factor that keeps anything antithetical to the capitalist world-view away from the eyes and ears of a large chunk of the population has been mass media.8 Over the years, the pursuit to maximise profit by any means has, along with the vested interests that control it, made the media – radio, television, newspapers, even a large part of online media – present to its viewers only the most sensationalist, head-turning, eye-catching content, while the real social problems are either completely ignored or deliberately played down. The media has also campaigned both overtly and subliminally against any and all anti-capitalist – especially socialist – thought and this has led to a situation where the working masses, even in the face of starvation, will blame each other for their plight, but never the ruling elite.

The elite, however, a small number of people who hoard most of the world’s wealth, are actively working to maintain the status quo, even in the face of global calamities – wildfires, floods, droughts, hurricanes – which have their origin in a climate that is slowly changing for the worse. 124 years after Arrhenius’s paper and forty years after the first World Climate Conference, we are nowhere near keeping greenhouse gas levels low enough to avoid catastrophic global warming and climate breakdown. Close to the end of the 20th century, major corporations actively funded research that argued climate change was either not real, or not as dangerous as it was made to look.9 Public pressure has since reduced such incidents, but corporations continue to covertly spread climate change denial, while governments continue to help them burn more and more fossil fuel, the primary source of greenhouse gases today. If things stay as they are now, which seems likely, global warming coupled with positive feedback loops will soon result in worldwide climate disaster in a few decades.10

A very large number of people believe that technology can save us from the problems we are facing. But this is just wishful thinking. Technology is not magic. It takes energy to build, operate and maintain technological systems. At the beginning of the oil boom nearly 150 years ago, the United States, the then sole producer of oil, produced (a better word would be ‘extracted’) only around 2,000 barrels of oil in a year. By 1906, it had climbed to 2,400 barrels every 10 minutes. Today, the world extracts 2,800 barrels of oil every 3 seconds.11 Not only are we using more oil, we are also using more oil to extract and process oil itself, as the accessibility and quality of the black liquid slowly deteriorate. While it may still be viable to switch to renewables, even if we ignore the fact that the process itself is expected to use a large amount of oil, meeting the demands of the world economy as it stands today is virtually impossible, since there is not enough renewable energy to go around. Further, the infrastructure necessary for such a society would mean that we would have to overhaul entire systems just to begin the widespread use of renewables. Diminishing returns on energy, depletion of oil reserves, dangerous levels of global warming, and difficulties in shifting to renewables are together creating a predicament of global societal collapse.12 While some level of collapse is certain, the actions we take in the immediate future will mean the difference between billions of deaths and millions.

From ancient history to the Paris Commune, and then on to this very day, people have fought for self-determination and against unjustified authority. In a world where freedom and democracy are hollow words thrown around by imperial powers to justify neocolonialist interventions and destruction of governments elected by the people, we should be wise enough to not be fooled by the facade created by mainstream media and the popular narrative. Armed with the tools of logic, reason and action, we the common people of the world shall prevail. Democracy shall prevail. Justice shall prevail. Humanity shall prevail.

Long Live the People’s Revolution.
